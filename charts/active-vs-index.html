Source: S&P Indices Versus Active (SPIVA) Scorecards. Numbers above each bar show the asset-weighted average return of all US domestic active equity funds minus the S&P 500 return for that year. Negative values mean the average active fund lagged the index.

The SPIVA data from 2004 through 2025 reveals a sobering reality for active management: in most years, the majority of professional stock-pickers fail to beat the S&P 500. This underperformance isn't just a streak of bad luck; it is a structural challenge. As the tables show, even in "up" years like 2024 and 2025, over 65% to 79% of large-cap funds lagged behind the index.

This data validates Warren Buffett’s long-standing advice that retail investors should favor low-cost index funds. When only about 30% of individual stocks beat the index itself—as seen in 2025—finding the winning minority is statistically improbable for most. By "owning the haystack" through an index fund, investors capture the total market return without paying for expensive expertise that rarely pays for itself.
