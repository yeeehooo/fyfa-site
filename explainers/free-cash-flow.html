Free cash flow

Free Cash Flow (FCF) is the actual cash a company has left over after paying all its operating expenses and the costs required to maintain or expand its physical assets.

FCF=Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures

Free cash flow is the ultimate reality check for investors because, unlike accounting earnings, it cannot be easily manipulated by clever bookkeeping. It represents the "discretionary" money a company can use to pay dividends, buy back shares, or acquire competitors without needing to borrow more money. A company with high earnings but negative FCF is often in trouble, as it is burning more cash to stay alive than it is actually bringing in.

A powerful example of the importance of FCF is the turnaround of Microsoft under Satya Nadella. While the company was already profitable, the shift toward a cloud-based subscription model (Azure) transformed its FCF profile by creating a massive, recurring stream of cash that required relatively low maintenance capital compared to traditional software manufacturing. By 2025, Microsoft's ability to generate over $70 billion in annual free cash flow allowed it to make massive investments in AI startups and return billions to shareholders simultaneously, proving that cash—not just profit—is the lifeblood of growth.

To evaluate a business correctly, you must look past the "paper profits" on the income statement and verify that the company is actually putting cash in the bank.

The most resilient investments are found in companies that can generate plenty of cash while continuing to grow their operations.
